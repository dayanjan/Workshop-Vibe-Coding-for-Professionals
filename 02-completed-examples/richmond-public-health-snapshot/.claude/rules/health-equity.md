## Health Equity Standards

These rules apply to all code, narratives, visualizations, and documentation in this project.

### Language

- ALWAYS use person-first language in all outputs:
  - "people with diabetes" — not "diabetics"
  - "people experiencing obesity" — not "obese people"
  - "people who smoke" — not "smokers"
  - "people with hypertension" — not "hypertensives"
  - "people experiencing housing instability" — not "the homeless"
  - "people with limited income" — not "the poor"

- NEVER use deficit-based framing. Frame findings as opportunities for investment and improvement:
  - "Greatest opportunity for diabetes prevention" — not "worst diabetes rate"
  - "Community with the most to gain from expanded access" — not "most underserved area"

- Frame disparities as the result of systemic and structural factors (historical disinvestment, policy environments, infrastructure gaps), not as characteristics of the people living in those areas.

### Data Integrity

- NEVER draw causal conclusions from cross-sectional ecological data. Use "associated with," "coincides with," or "occurs alongside" — never "causes," "leads to," or "results in."

- NEVER compare age-adjusted rates and crude rates in the same chart or table without clearly labeling each. When possible, use one rate type consistently within a single analysis.

- ALWAYS note that CDC PLACES data are model-based small area estimates produced via multilevel regression and poststratification (MRP) on BRFSS data. They are not direct survey results and carry model uncertainty.

- Provide structural context when comparing jurisdictions. Richmond City is an independent city with a different tax base, governance structure, and historical investment pattern than the surrounding counties.

- NEVER attribute area-level data to individuals. Area-level prevalence estimates describe populations within a geography, not any specific person living there. Always note the ecological fallacy when interpreting geographic comparisons.

### Data Sources

- All data must come from publicly available sources (CDC PLACES API, Census ACS API).
- No proprietary, paywalled, or restricted-access data in this project.
